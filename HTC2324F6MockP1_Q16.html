Question 16

Let α and β be real numbers such that {αβ=1000,4log⁡β=3(log⁡α)2−3.

  1. Find the possible values of α and β. (3 marks)
  2. α and β are the first term and the second term of a geometric sequence respectively. If the sum to infinity of the sequence is S, find S. (2 marks)

Solution

Part (a)

From the first equation αβ=1000, take the common logarithm (log10) on both sides:

log⁡(αβ)=log⁡1000 log⁡α+log⁡β=3…(1)
Concept Note: Remember that log⁡(xy)=log⁡x+log⁡y and log⁡1000=log⁡103=3. This transformation is crucial to link the first equation with the second equation which involves logarithmic terms.

From (1), we have log⁡β=3−log⁡α. Substitute this into the second given equation:

4(3−log⁡α)=3(log⁡α)2−3 12−4log⁡α=3(log⁡α)2−3

Rearrange the terms to form a quadratic equation in terms of log⁡α:

3(log⁡α)2+4log⁡α−15=0

Let u=log⁡α. Then 3u2+4u−15=0. Factorizing the quadratic:

(3u−5)(u+3)=0 u=53oru=−3
Common Trap: Don't stop at finding u (or log⁡α). The question asks for the values of α and β. You must convert back from the logarithm form.

Case 1: When log⁡α=53

α=105/3 log⁡β=3−53=43⟹β=104/3

Case 2: When log⁡α=−3

α=10−3 log⁡β=3−(−3)=6⟹β=106

Thus, the possible values are:

{α=105/3β=104/3or{α=10−3β=106

Part (b)

Let a be the first term and r be the common ratio.

a=α,r=βα

For the sum to infinity S to exist, we must have |r|<1. Let's check the two cases found in (a).

Check Case 1: α=105/3,β=104/3

r=104/3105/3=104/3−5/3=10−1/3=1101/3

Since 101/3>1, we have 0<r<1. Thus, |r|<1 holds. The sum to infinity exists.

Check Case 2: α=10−3,β=106

r=10610−3=106−(−3)=109

Since 109>1, the sum to infinity does NOT exist for this case.

Common Trap: Students often calculate the sum for both cases without checking the condition for convergence (|r|<1). Calculating S for Case 2 would lead to a mark deduction.

Calculate S using the valid case (Case 1):

S=a1−r=105/31−10−1/3

To simplify (optional but good for clarity):

S=105/31−1101/3=105/3101/3−1101/3=105/3⋅101/3101/3−1=102101/3−1=100103−1

(Leaving the answer as 105/31−10−1/3 is usually acceptable unless exact surd form is required, but exact form is preferred).

Note for Question 16: Solving systems involving logs often requires converting multiplication inside the log to addition outside (log⁡αβ→log⁡α+log⁡β). For geometric sequences, the validity of the Sum to Infinity is a frequent discriminator in Section B questions.

Consolidated Notes & Strategy

Notes
  • Log Laws: log⁡(xy)=log⁡x+log⁡y.
  • Geometric Sequence: n-th term Tn=arn−1. Sum to infinity S=a1−r.
  • Convergence Condition: Sum to infinity exists if and only if −1<r<1.
Strategy
  • Part (a): Transform the non-linear system into a linear/quadratic form by substituting variables (e.g., u=log⁡α). Always find the values of the original variables (α,β) at the end.
  • Part (b): Calculate the common ratio r for all possible pairs found in (a). Reject the case where |r|≥1 before calculating the sum.
Required Skill
  • Solving quadratic equations via substitution.
  • Properties of Logarithms.
  • Geometric Sequence formulas and convergence checks.
