  1. John and Mary take turns to throw a fair die until one of them gets a number `1' or `6'. John throws the die first. Find the probability that John gets a number `6'.

Detailed Solution

We want the probability that John gets a number 6 in this game, where John and Mary take turns throwing a fair die (John first), and the game stops once someone gets a 1 or 6.

Step 1: How can John win? John can only win on his own turns. He might win: on his 1st turn, or on his 2nd turn, or on his 3rd turn, \dots We analyse these cases one by one and then recognise a geometric pattern.

Case 1: John wins on his 1st turn On Johns first throw: P(John throws 6)=16. So the probability that John wins on his 1st turn is 16.

Case 2: John wins on his 2nd turn For John to reach his 2nd turn and then win:
  • Johns first throw must be 2,3,4 or 5;
  • Marys first throw must be 2,3,4 or 5;
  • then Johns second throw must be 6.
Concept Note: Calculating "Continuation" Probability The game continues only if the number thrown is NOT 1 and NOT 6.
The set of numbers that continue the game is {2,3,4,5}.
Probability = 46=23.
Each non-1-or-6 throw (i.e. 2,3,4,5) has probability 46=23, and throwing 6 has probability 16. Therefore, the probability that John wins on his 2nd turn is (23)(23)(16)=(23)216=16(49).

General pattern: John wins on his k-th turn To reach Johns k-th turn, there are 2(k1) previous throws (John, Mary, John, Mary, ), and all of them must be from {2,3,4,5}. The probability of this is (23)2(k1). Then John must throw 6 on his k-th turn, which has probability 16. Hence, the probability that John wins on his k-th turn is (23)2(k1)16=16(49)k1.

Exam Notes & Strategy

Common Trap: "Stopping" vs "Winning" Students often confuse the condition to stop the game ("1 or 6") with the condition for John to win ("6").
  • If John throws a 1: The game stops, but he does not win.
  • If John throws a 6: The game stops, and he wins.
  • If John throws 2, 3, 4, 5: The game continues.
Always calculate the probability of the game continuing (which is 4/6=2/3) correctly.
Alternative Strategy: System of Equations Let P be the probability John wins.
John wins immediately with prob 16.
If John throws 2,3,4,5 (prob 46), it becomes Mary's turn. By symmetry, from Mary's perspective, her winning probability is exactly the same as John's initial state if the game restarts. However, we need to be careful: if Mary throws 2,3,4,5, the game returns to John.
A simpler recursive view: P=16+46(Probability Mary throws non-winning/non-stopping)P This method is often more complex for this specific question type than the geometric series method, but useful to know for "infinite states".

Strategy

  2. Identify Pattern: Look for the common ratio between consecutive winning cases.

  4. Double Check: Ensure the common ratio r represents "John fails AND Mary fails" (two turns passing).
