Question 16

The box-and-whisker diagram below shows the distribution of the scores of students in a test. It is given that the scores of Peter and Carol are equal to the median and the lower quartile of the distribution respectively. The standard scores of Peter and Carol are 0.4 and 1.6 respectively.

  1. Find the standard deviation of the distribution. (2 marks)
  2. A student, Sam, claims that if the score of a candidate is greater than his score by 30, then the difference between their standard scores exceeds 2.5. Is the claim correct? Explain your answer. (2 marks)
Solution

(a)

Let be the mean and be the standard deviation of the scores.

From the box-and-whisker diagram, we read:

  • Peter's score (xP) = Median = 50
  • Carol's score (xC) = Lower Quartile = 25
Concept Note: Reading the Box-and-Whisker Diagram To solve this, look at the diagram provided in the question:
  • The Median is the vertical line inside the rectangular box.
  • The Lower Quartile is the vertical line at the left edge of the rectangular box.

Using the standard score formula z=x:

For Peter: 50=0.4 ......(1)

For Carol: 25=1.6 ......(2)

Subtract equation (2) from equation (1) to eliminate :

5025=0.4(1.6)

5025=2.0

25=2

=12.5

The standard deviation is 12.5.

(b)

Let Sam's score be xS and the candidate's score be x.

Given: xxS=30.

We need to check the difference in their standard scores (zdiff).

zdiff=xxS

zdiff=xxS

zdiff=30

Concept Note: Standard Score Difference The difference in standard scores depends only on the difference in raw scores and the standard deviation. The mean () is irrelevant here.

Substitute =12.5 from part (a):

zdiff=3012.5

zdiff=2.4

Conclusion:

Since 2.4<2.5, the difference between their standard scores does not exceed 2.5.

Thus, the claim is incorrect.

Common Trap: Don't try to find the actual value of the mean (). It cancels out in both parts. Focus only on the difference between the scores.
